Research

Understanding Intergroup Bias

Advisor: Junyi Jessy Li; Mentor: Venkata S Govindarajan; University of Texas at Austin

In this project, we aim to understand the cues in social comments that tell which category - ingroup, outgroup, or third-party, a particular entity belongs to. We scrape more than 5 million Reddit comments from the NFL Football game threads and mask/highlight team name mentions. We then feed in the processed comments to gpt4 and set up the MTurk for human annotation. We are at the stage of analyzing results and improving the task design, interpreting how contextual information affects the task performance. Meanwhile, we resort to co-reference resolution to match pronouns with team names (for more entities) and plan to train a model specialized at the task.

May 2023 - Present

Would I Lie to You

Advisor: Amanda Stent; Coworker: Tahiya Chowdhury; Colby College

We introduce a multimodal dataset containing multi-party deceptive and truthful conversation in a public environment during the British TV show `Would I Lie to You', where participants compete in teams to guess truthful and deceptive statements of others. We curate the speech events from publicly available videos of the show via speaker segmentation and speech recognition. Using verbal and non-verbal behaviors of the speakers through acoustic, linguistic, and visual features, we explored classification for deception detection. The experiments show high-performing classification results using verbal only, non-verbal only, and using both when compared to the human baseline. This work has implications for real-world deception in multi-party conversations and can inform about cues used by humans for trust and deception in multi-party settings.

May 2022 - present

Examining the Cost of Similarity in Identifier Naming

Advisor: Naser Al Madi; Colby College

This work examines the cost of identifier similarity in orthography (word shape), phonology (pronunciation), or semantic (meaning) representation and how that affects debugging performance and programmer workload. We design three debugging tasks in Java and recruit 40+ CS majors to finish the tasks with eye tracking equipment tracking their eyeball movements. By analyzing participants success of debugging, time usage and recognizing common identifier naming combinations that hinder code comprehension, we can discover new programming best-practices and create automated tools that flag problematic naming combinations.
