Intergroup Bias dynamics & discourse structure

Advisor: Junyi Jessy Li; University of Texas at Austin

This project is a part of DREU. Specifically, I studied intergroup bias on social media. Utilizing praw, I scraped seven million Reddit comments from the past two NFL game seasons. I then identified and masked all team names and their pronouns (through coreference resolution), scripted an Amazon Mechanical Turk interface for human annotation, and tested the task on GPT-4 using few-shot learning. Our findings highlight challenges in intergroup classification by both humans and foundational language models. Given this, we examine linguistic cues and target at training task-specific language models for accurate intergroup tagging, drawing inspiration from NER and POS tagging.

Paper in preparation for EMNLP, 2024

Would I Lie to You

Advisor: Amanda Stent; Collaborator: Tahiya Chowdhury; Colby College

In this project, I curated a multi-party, real-world dataset from the British TV show 'Would I Lie to You' for multimodal deception detection study. Using tools like diart, wav2vec, and Prodigy, I segmented, transcribed, and annotated target statements in the video. We then extracted multimodal features from the dataset, encompassing acoustic cues such as prosody, linguistic features including N-grams and GloVe embeddings, as well as visual features from the DeepFace. To demonstrate the effectiveness of the dataset, we first took an early-fusion approach and trained multiple machine/deep learning models. For comparison purposes, we also explored Perceiver IO that leverages a later-fusion approach of multimodal data. Our best model of BLSTM trained on verbal-only features achieved an impressive accuracy of 96%. Meanwhile, the early-fusion approach works better in our setting, and the additional visual features fail to augment model performance.

Examining the Cost of Similarity in Identifier Naming

Advisor: Naser Al Madi; Colby College

This work examines the cost of identifier similarity in orthography (word shape), phonology (pronunciation), or semantic (meaning) representation and how that affects debugging performance and programmer workload. We design three debugging tasks in Java and recruit 40+ CS majors to finish the tasks with eye tracking equipment tracking their eyeball movements. By analyzing participants success of debugging, time usage and recognizing common identifier naming combinations that hinder code comprehension, we can discover new programming best-practices and create automated tools that flag problematic naming combinations.

Paper published at PPIG, 2022.

Term Paper on the Primitive Element Theorem

MATH1530, Abstract Algebra; Instructor: Madeline Brandt; Brown University

The primitive element theorem states that a finite extension is simple if and only if there are finitely many intermediary fields between the original and the extended field. In this term paper, I introduced necessary definitions and propositions, along with concrete examples, to build up toward the theorem.
